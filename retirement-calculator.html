Inflation rate
The average annual rate at which prices rise. This erodes the purchasing power of your money over time — a $5,000/month budget today might need $9,000/month in 30 years at 2% inflation, or $12,000 at 3%. The tool adjusts all projections for inflation automatically.
Typical value: 2.5%–3.5% for most developed countries. Use 4–5% if you're concerned about persistent inflation.

Cost basis
The percentage of your brokerage portfolio that represents your original contributions (not gains). Only the growth portion is taxed — the basis is returned to you tax-free. A 40% basis means 60% of the account is gains. Cost basis explained →

Investment Return Rate (IRR)
IRR is the average annual return you expect from your investment portfolio in each period. A 7% IRR means your portfolio grows by an average of 7% per year. In early retirement you might hold more equities (higher return, higher risk); later you typically shift to bonds and cash (lower return, lower risk). The calculator uses each period's rate to project your portfolio accurately rather than assuming one flat rate for 40+ years.
Typical values: 7–9% early retirement (equity-heavy) · 4–6% mid-retirement · 2–4% late retirement (conservative)

⚠ SEQUENCE OF RETURNS RISK — What is this? →

Two portfolios with identical average returns can have very different outcomes depending on the order those returns arrive. If markets crash in your first years of retirement and you're withdrawing to live on, you're selling more units at low prices — a hole the portfolio may never fully recover from, even when markets rebound.

This tool uses the 5 IRR values you entered above to run two scenarios side-by-side: your plan as entered, and a worst-case version where the same returns arrive in reverse order (bad periods first). Any difference in outcome is pure sequence risk — not a different average return.

Safe Withdrawal Rate (SWR)
The safe withdrawal rate is your planned year-1 withdrawal expressed as a percentage of your total nest egg. A 4% SWR on a $1M portfolio means withdrawing $40,000 in year 1, then adjusting for inflation each year. Lower is safer — a 3% rate gives your portfolio far more buffer against bad market sequences than a 6% rate.
Typical value: 3–4% is considered conservative and sustainable over 30+ years. Above 5% carries meaningful depletion risk.

Why the two lines may overlap or diverge
The amber line shows your portfolio if you withdraw exactly your planned annual amount (adjusted for inflation each year). The dashed line shows what happens if you withdraw the mathematical maximum the portfolio can sustain — right up to your legacy target at the end. When both lines are identical, it means your planned spending already equals the maximum sustainable amount. When the dashed line sits higher, you could afford to spend more. The gap between them is your financial buffer.

Monte Carlo Simulation
Monte Carlo simulation
A Monte Carlo simulation runs your retirement plan thousands of times, each time with slightly different random annual returns drawn from a realistic distribution. Instead of a single projected outcome, you get a range — showing the 10th, 25th, 50th, 75th and 90th percentile results. The "success rate" is simply the percentage of runs where your portfolio survived to your target age. Monte Carlo explained →
A success rate above 85–90% is generally considered robust. Below 70% suggests your plan needs adjustment.
Each run draws log-normally distributed annual returns where the geometric mean equals your IRR input — so the MC and deterministic models share the same effective return assumption. σ controls the year-to-year volatility around that geometric mean.
